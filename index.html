I am a Ph.D. candidate in Environmental and Water Resources Engineering working with Prof. Emily Kumpel. My research spans drinking water quality and access, environmental determinants of public health, and the application of machine learning to water infrastructure, across both the United States and South Asia.

My dissertation, From Household to Infrastructure: Empirical and Computational Approaches to Drinking Water Equity, brings together field-based household surveys in Indian cities and computational methods to understand how water systems shape public health and daily life. In the US, I work with the Massachusetts Department of Environmental Protection on lead in schools and lead service line identification.

Before UMass, I trained in population studies, development studies, and agricultural engineering in India. This interdisciplinary background shapes how I approach water as a question of access, equity, and lived experience, not just infrastructure.

Research

My research addresses drinking water quality, reliability, and equity across the United States and South Asia. I combine field-based household surveys, large-scale data collection, and computational methods, including machine learning and spatial analysis, to understand how water infrastructure shapes public health and community well-being.

Intermittent water supply

IWS unpredictability and health outcomes (Hubli-Dharwad): Using longitudinal data from nearly 2,000 households, this study examined how water delivery unpredictability is associated with waterborne disease. Households where water arrived on a different day than expected had 47% higher odds of reported illness compared to those with a predictable supply. This paper is currently under review at Nature Water (preprint available on ResearchSquare).

IWS reliability and characterization (multi-city survey, ongoing): I designed and led field data collection across Mumbai, Nashik, and Solapur, administering approximately 600 household surveys on supply schedules, predictability, and coping strategies across diverse urban contexts in India.

Machine learning & water infrastructure

Identifying lead service lines using machine learning

Lead service lines remain a significant source of lead contamination in drinking water systems across the United States. As part of the MassDEP Water-Smart Schools Program, I am developing and evaluating guideline-based machine learning methods that can identify lead service lines from administrative and infrastructure data, supporting regulatory compliance and remediation efforts in Massachusetts.

Remote sensing & environmental systems

Satellite data for drought resilience and policy

As a Graduate Fellow with the Data Science for the Public Good program at the UVA Biocomplexity Institute, I applied satellite remote sensing to analyze drought resilience in the Sahel and inform economic policy decisions in Zimbabwe. This work resulted in two technical reports and publicly available data tools.

Unpredictability of intermittent water supply and effects on child health in Hubli-Dharwad, India
Tajanpure, P., Kumpel, E., & Ercumen, A.
Revised & resubmitted — Nature Water Preprint: ResearchSquare (2025) →

In many cities across South Asia, piped water arrives not continuously but on an unpredictable schedule, sometimes hours late, sometimes days. Using longitudinal survey data from nearly 2,000 households in Hubli-Dharwad, India, this study found that households where water arrived on a different day than expected had 47% higher odds of reported illness compared to those with a predictable supply schedule. Effects were strongest among households receiving water infrequently. These findings suggest that improving schedule reliability within existing intermittent systems could be a practical and immediate pathway to reducing waterborne disease in cities where full upgrades to continuous supply are not yet feasible.
