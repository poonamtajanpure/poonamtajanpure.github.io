I am a Ph.D. candidate working with Prof. Emily Kumpel on questions at the intersection of water infrastructure, public health, and equity.

My dissertation — From Household to Infrastructure: Empirical and Computational Approaches to Drinking Water Equity — draws on field research across Indian cities and machine learning methods to understand how water systems shape the health and daily lives of the people they serve.

Before UMass, I trained in population studies and development studies in Mumbai, and in agricultural engineering in Rahuri — a background that shapes how I think about water not just as an engineering problem, but as a question of access, equity, and lived experience.

Research

I study how water infrastructure shapes health and daily life — particularly in cities where piped water arrives intermittently. My work combines household surveys, field data collection, and computational methods including machine learning and spatial analysis.

Intermittent water supply

IWS unpredictability and child health (Hubli-Dharwad): Using longitudinal data from nearly 2,000 households, this study examined how IWS delivery unpredictability affects diarrhea in children under five. Children in households where water arrived on a different day than expected had 47% higher odds of diarrhea. This paper is currently under review at Nature Water (preprint available on ResearchSquare).

IWS reliability and characterization (multi-city survey, ongoing): In this project we designed field data collection across Mumbai, Nashik, and Solapur, administering approximately 600 household surveys on supply schedules, predictability, and coping strategies across diverse urban contexts in India.

Machine learning & water infrastructure

Identifying lead service lines using machine learning

Lead service lines are a major source of lead exposure in drinking water, particularly for children. As part of the MassDEP Water-Smart Schools Program, I am developing and evaluating guideline-based machine learning methods that can identify lead service lines from administrative and infrastructure data.

Remote sensing & resilience

Satellite data for drought resilience and policy

As a Graduate Fellow with the Data Science for the Public Good program at the UVA Biocomplexity Institute, I applied satellite remote sensing to analyze drought resilience in the Sahel and inform economic policy decisions in Zimbabwe. This work contributed to two technical reports and live data tools.

Unpredictability of intermittent water supply and effects on child health in Hubli-Dharwad, India
Tajanpure, P., Kumpel, E., & Ercumen, A.
Revised & resubmitted — Nature Water Preprint: ResearchSquare (2025) →

In many cities across South Asia, piped water arrives not continuously but on an unpredictable schedule — sometimes hours late, sometimes days. Using longitudinal survey data from nearly 2,000 households in Hubli-Dharwad, India, this study found that children in households where water arrived on a different day than expected had 47% higher odds of diarrhea compared to households with a predictable supply schedule. Effects were strongest among households receiving water infrequently. These findings suggest that improving schedule reliability, even within existing intermittent systems, could be a low-cost, immediate pathway to reducing waterborne disease in cities where full upgrades to continuous supply are decades away.
