I am a Ph.D. candidate in Civil and Environmental Engineering at the University of Massachusetts Amherst, where I work with Prof. Emily Kumpel on questions at the intersection of water infrastructure, public health, and equity.

My dissertation — From Household to Infrastructure: Empirical and Computational Approaches to Drinking Water Equity — draws on field research across Indian cities and machine learning methods to understand how water systems shape the health and daily lives of the people they serve, especially children.

Before UMass, I trained in population studies and development studies in Mumbai, and in agricultural engineering in Rahuri — a background that shapes how I think about water not just as an engineering problem, but as a question of access, equity, and lived experience.

My work sits at the boundary of environmental engineering, public health, and data science. I use a combination of field-based methods — household surveys, spatial sampling, field data collection — and computational tools including machine learning and statistical modeling to study how water infrastructure affects health and equity.

Current projects
Intermittent water supply & health

Water unpredictability and child health in urban India

Millions of households in India receive water through intermittent piped systems — taps that run for only a few hours a day, on unpredictable schedules. My research asks: what does this unpredictability mean for children's health? Working with collaborators at UMass Amherst and NC State, I conducted household surveys across Hubli-Dharwad and led a follow-up multi-city survey (Mumbai, Nashik, Solapur) covering approximately 600 households, examining supply patterns, coping strategies, and socioeconomic impacts. The Hubli-Dharwad paper is currently under review at Nature Water.

Machine learning & water infrastructure

Identifying lead service lines using machine learning

Lead service lines — pipes connecting the water main to homes — are a major source of lead exposure in drinking water, particularly for children. Replacing them requires knowing where they are, which is often poorly documented. As part of the MassDEP Water-Smart Schools Program, I am developing and evaluating guideline-based machine learning methods that can identify lead service lines from administrative and infrastructure data. This work bridges regulatory data management and computational experimentation.

Remote sensing & resilience

Satellite data for drought resilience and policy

As a Graduate Fellow with the Data Science for the Public Good program at the UVA Biocomplexity Institute, I applied satellite remote sensing to analyze drought resilience in the Sahel and support economic policy decisions in Zimbabwe. This work introduced me to the potential — and the limits — of earth observation data for understanding environmental vulnerability in low-resource settings.
